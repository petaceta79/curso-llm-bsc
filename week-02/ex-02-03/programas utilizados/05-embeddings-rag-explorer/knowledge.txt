Acme Robotics is a fictional company founded in 2019 in Girona, Catalonia. It
designs and manufactures small autonomous warehouse robots. Its headquarters are
in Girona, with a second assembly plant in Lleida opened in 2023.

The flagship product is the Pallet Pup, a knee-high mobile robot that ferries
pallets between shelving and loading docks. The Pallet Pup has a top speed of 2.4
metres per second and a payload capacity of 600 kilograms. Its battery lasts nine
hours on a single charge and recharges to full in about 90 minutes.

The Pallet Pup finds its way using LiDAR together with floor markers, and it runs
on an in-house operating system called PupOS. PupOS receives an over-the-air
update roughly once a month, and every update is signed so a robot will refuse
firmware that is not from Acme.

Acme's second product is the Shelf Owl, a small camera drone that flies the aisles
at night and counts stock on the shelves. The Shelf Owl shares the PupOS software
stack with the Pallet Pup, which is why a warehouse can manage both from one
dashboard. A Shelf Owl flies for 35 minutes per charge, photographs about 1,800
shelf locations per night, and lands itself on its charging perch when the battery
drops below 15 percent.

The third product is the Nest, a floor-level fast-charging dock. A Pallet Pup that
docks on a Nest reaches 80 percent charge in 25 minutes, and a warehouse typically
installs one Nest for every four robots.

Acme Robotics employs 140 people. Its CEO is Berta Comas, a former logistics
engineer who co-founded the company. The head of engineering is Nil Bass. The
company's motto is "small robots, heavy lifting."

In 2024 Acme shipped 3,200 Pallet Pup units, mostly to fruit and textile
warehouses across southern Europe. Italy and the south of France were the two
largest markets that year.

A typical installation takes two days: one day to glue and calibrate the floor
markers, and one day for the robots to map the aisles and rehearse their routes at
low speed. No changes to shelving are needed, and the warehouse keeps operating
during the install.

Acme's largest deployment is a citrus warehouse near Valencia, where a fleet of 46
Pallet Pups and 6 Shelf Owls moves about 900 pallets per day in the picking
season. The fleet there replaced two diesel forklifts, and the customer reports
that picking errors dropped by a third in the first year.

All the robots in a warehouse are coordinated from PupHub, the fleet dashboard.
PupHub assigns jobs, balances battery levels across the fleet, and produces a
daily report with pallets moved, kilometres driven, and near-stop events. It runs
on a small server inside the warehouse, and it keeps working if the internet
connection goes down.

Acme runs a two-day training course called the Acme Academy for warehouse staff.
The first day covers daily operation and the emergency procedures; the second day
covers reading PupHub reports and swapping a battery. Every support contract
includes two Academy seats per year.

A Pallet Pup operates in temperatures from minus 5 to 40 degrees Celsius and is
rated IP54 against dust and splashing water. It is not designed for freezer
warehouses; Acme has announced a cold-store version, the Pallet Pup Arctic, for
2027.

A Pallet Pup is sold with a two-year warranty that covers parts and labour.
Batteries are treated as a consumable and are covered separately for twelve
months. Acme offers a support contract that adds next-business-day on-site repair.

The list price of a single Pallet Pup is 18,900 euros. Fleets of ten or more
receive a volume discount, and the support contract is priced per robot per year.
The Shelf Owl costs 6,400 euros, and the Nest charging dock costs 2,100 euros.

Every Pallet Pup ships with an emergency stop that any nearby worker can press,
and the robot is certified to the European machinery safety standard for
collaborative mobile robots. It slows automatically when a person is detected
within two metres.
